In this work we investigate a class of random walks that interacts with its environment called Tree Builder Random Walk (TBRW). In our settings, at each step, the walker adds a random number of vertices to its position sampled according to a distribution \(Q\). Previous works showed that the walker is ballistic with a well-defined speed, and that the TBRW admits a renewal structure, meaning that the process can be split into i.i.d epochs. We show that the first renewal time has exponential tail. Moreover, we leverage this light tail and also show a large deviation result for the distance of the walker from the root.

Introduction

We investigate a class of random walks in which the walker interacts with its environment by modifying the underlying graph. In this model, the walker can add a random number of vertices to its position as it walks. This ability to alter the structure of the environment introduces a feedback mechanism, distinguishing this class of random walk from the classical Random Walk on Random Environment model [cite ??] and from more recent models where the walker changes its environment but keeping the underlying graph structure unchanged, here we have reinforced random walks (cite ??). In random walk theory, one of the main questions regards transience/recurrence of the walker. When it comes to transient random walks, one possible reason for the walker not visit its initial position infinitely often is that it ``escapes to infinity''. That is, its distance from its initial position goes to infinity. Notice that notion of distance comes naturally from the graph structure of the environment, then we can also quantify ``how fast'' the walker escapes to infinity by introducing a notion of speed at a given time which is naturally defined as $$ \frac{{\text{distance of the walker from a fixed point at time }n}}{n}. $$ The notion of speed lead us to class of ballistic random walks. A random walk is ballistic if \liminf_{n\to \infty}\frac{{\text{distance of the walker from fixedpoint at time }n}}{n} >0, almost surely. Ballistic walkers are moving away from their initial position at a linear speed.

Not all transient walkers are ballistic, the classical example being the simple symmetrical random walk on \(\mathbb{Z}^d\) for \(d\ge 3\). In this case, the walker can wander too much over the environment, exhibiting a diffusive behavior. The relation between transience and ballisticity can be a truly hard problem. In the classical theory of RWRE, the question whether directional transience implies ballisticity, even in the most standard settings, remains open since the influential work of Solomon in the 1970's and the works of Sniztman in the end of the 90s. In [??] Sniztman conjectured that for RWRE in \(\mathbb{Z}^d\), a directional random walk on a random variable under the so-called uniformly elliptic condition is ballistic.

Before we define the renewal structure, let us say a couple words about the markovian nature of the TBRW. Observe that the TBRW itself is a Markov process on the space of rooted trees, even though each of its components separately are not. In some proofs, it will be useful to make use of the (Strong) Markov Properties. For that, we will denote the shift operator of length \(n\) by \(\theta_n\). In light of the Markov Properties, it is important to point out that when the TBRW is shifted by \(n\) steps and \(\mathcal{L}_n = Q\) for all \(n\), the shifted process is a TBRW that starts from a random initial state \((T_n,X_n)\) and with the exact same sequence of laws \(\{\mathcal{L}_n\}_n\). More formally, if \(\phi: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}\) is \(\mathcal{F}\)-measurable, then the Simple Markov Property works as follows $$ E_{T_0,X_0;Q}\left [\phi\circ \theta_n \; \middle | \; \mathcal{F}_n\right] = E_{T_n,X_n;Q}\left [\phi\right]. $$
