  • Dots are vertices, connections are edges
  • The degree of a vertex is the number of neighbors it has
  • The distance between two vertices is the length (number of edges) of the shortest path connecting them;
  • The diameter is the maximum distance, that is, $\max_{u,v}{\rm dist}(u,v)$;

  • Graph theory traces its roots to the 18th century.
  • Founded by the Swiss mathematician, Leonhard Euler.
  • Started with the solution to the Königsberg Bridge Problem in 1736.

The city of Königsberg in Prussia (now Kaliningrad, Russia) was set on both sides of the Pregel River, and included two large islands which were connected to each other, and to the two mainland portions of the city, by seven bridges. The problem was to walk through the city in a way that would cross each of those bridges once and only once.

  • reaching an island or mainland bank other than via one of the bridges, or
  • accessing any bridge without crossing to its other end are explicitly unacceptable.

  • The seven bridges problem is known as the first time graphs appeared in math
  • The real breakthrough was the reformulation of the problem in simpler terms

In mathematics, and more specifically in graph theory, a graph is a structure consisting of a set of dots connected by lines. The dots are called vertices (or nodes) and the connections between them are called edges (or links).

  • Vertices (or nodes): The fundamental units or points of a graph.
  • Edges (or links): The connections between pairs of vertices.
  • A graph can be directed or undirected, depending on whether the edges have a direction.
  • Graphs are used to model pairwise relations between objects.
